Lesson 1 of 5

Follow the money

In healthcare, the person receiving care often does not directly pay the full cost. Money moves through a network of patients, providers, payers, and purchasers.

The core idea

To understand a healthtech business, ask three questions: who uses it, who pays for it, and what measurable outcome makes it worth paying for?

PurchaserFunds coverage
→
PayerAdministers benefits
↔
ProviderDelivers care & bills
↔
PatientReceives care & shares cost

Key financial relationships

PurchaserFunds coverage, often an employer or government.
PayerAdministers benefits and processes eligible claims; it may bear financial risk or act for the purchaser.
ProviderDelivers care and requests reimbursement.
PatientReceives care and may share its cost.

Several prices can describe one service

A healthcare service rarely has one simple price. A provider may publish a charge, negotiate a lower allowed amount with an insurer, receive a payment from the insurer, and bill the patient for a remaining share. The amount ultimately collected can also change after a claim is reviewed.

This makes cost difficult to predict at the moment a patient chooses care. Accurate estimates depend on the service, provider, insurance network, benefit design, and how much of the patient’s deductible has already been met.

Follow both value and risk

Financial riskWho loses money if care costs more than expected?
Clinical valueWhat health improvement is the spending intended to create?
Operational valueWhat time, capacity, or administrative work is improved?
EvidenceWhat data proves that the expected value occurred?
Real-world example

An employer purchases a health plan for employees. The insurer administers coverage, a clinic provides care, and the employee may pay a copay while the insurer pays the remaining approved amount.

Look beneath the transaction

A product can be free to the patient and still depend on a complex contract, eligibility rules, utilization targets, and proof of outcomes behind the scenes.

Why this matters for product designers

The most delightful user experience can still fail if it does not create value for the person or organization paying for it.

Designer takeawayIdentify the user, buyer, payer, and financial outcome before defining product success.
Lesson 2 of 5

Insurance changes the experience

Insurance spreads financial risk across a group and determines what services are covered, where people can receive care, and what they owe.

PremiumRegular coverage payment

Usually paid monthly by a person or employer

DeductibleAmount paid before the plan starts paying for many covered services

Some services may be covered before it is met

CopayFixed amount for a covered service

Often visible during booking or checkout

NetworkContracted providers

Affects access, choice, and cost

Other terms people encounter

CoinsuranceA percentage of a covered service cost paid by the patient.
Out-of-pocket maximumThe most a person pays in a plan year for covered in-network services; premiums and some other costs do not count.
Prior authorizationApproval required before some services are covered.
Explanation of benefitsA summary of how a claim was processed; it is not a bill.

Coverage is conditional, not binary

A service being “covered” does not necessarily mean it is free or automatically approved. Coverage may depend on clinical criteria, a referral, prior authorization, provider network status, visit limits, or the patient’s cost-sharing responsibilities.

Benefits also change over time. A patient may switch plans, an employer may change offerings, or a provider may leave a network. Product language should communicate what is known, what is estimated, and what still needs confirmation.

Moments where insurance affects experience

Finding careDetermining which providers and services are in network.
Choosing careComparing expected cost, access, and clinical fit.
Receiving careConfirming eligibility, referrals, and authorization.
After careUnderstanding claims, bills, denials, and appeals.
Real-world example

A patient may see a $1,000 service price, an insurer-negotiated price of $600, and owe only part of that amount depending on their deductible and coinsurance.

Design principle

Coverage and cost information must be understandable at the moment someone makes a care decision, not buried in insurance language.

Designer takeawayTranslate insurance complexity into clear, timely, and actionable guidance.
Lesson 3 of 5

How reimbursement works

After care is delivered, providers document the service and submit a claim so the payer can determine payment.

CareService delivered
→
CodeService documented
→
ClaimSent to payer
→
PaymentApproved or denied

What can happen to a claim

ApprovedThe payer accepts and reimburses the eligible amount.
DeniedThe payer declines payment and gives a reason.
AdjustedThe submitted and allowed amounts differ.
AppealedThe decision is challenged with more information.

A claim turns care into structured evidence

A payer was not present during the visit, so it relies on standardized information to decide whether and how much to pay. The claim identifies the patient, provider, diagnosis, service, location, and other details. Documentation in the medical record must support that structured request.

This process connects clinical work to financial work. A small documentation gap can create a denial, delay payment, generate follow-up tasks, and sometimes produce a confusing bill for the patient.

Common sources of reimbursement friction

EligibilityCoverage was inactive or could not be verified.
AuthorizationRequired approval was missing or incomplete.
CodingThe submitted code did not match rules or documentation.
CoordinationResponsibility between multiple payers was unclear.
Real-world example

If a clinician’s documentation does not support the submitted billing code, a claim may be denied, creating administrative work and delaying payment.

Designer prompt

Every missing detail can delay payment. Products must support accurate documentation without adding unnecessary burden to clinicians.

Designer takeawayDesign documentation workflows that make the right information easy to capture at the right time.
Lesson 4 of 5

Fee-for-service vs. value-based care

Payment models influence what organizations prioritize, measure, and build.

Fee-for-servicePayment for each visit, test, or procedure
Value-based carePayment linked to quality, outcomes, and cost
SubscriptionRecurring fee for access or service
Employer benefitCompany purchases access for employees

Payment models distribute incentives and risk

Fee-for-service generally rewards delivering more billable services. Value-based arrangements ask organizations to take more responsibility for quality and total cost, but the specific structure matters. Some models offer a bonus for meeting targets; others require the organization to absorb losses when costs are too high.

Many businesses combine models. A digital health company might charge an employer a recurring access fee, bill insurance for clinical visits, and earn additional payments when outcomes improve.

Questions to ask about a payment model

Unit of paymentIs payment tied to a visit, member, month, outcome, or contract?
Risk holderWho absorbs unexpected cost or poor performance?
Time horizonHow quickly must value appear to be rewarded?
Behavior encouragedWhat actions become more attractive under this model?
Real-world example

Under fee-for-service, a provider may be paid for each visit. Under a value-based arrangement, the organization may instead share savings for keeping a population healthier while meeting quality targets.

The design implication

Fee-for-service products may optimize throughput and documentation. Value-based products often emphasize prevention, coordination, engagement, and measurable outcomes.

Designer takeawayUnderstand the payment model because it shapes which behaviors and outcomes the product should support.
Lesson 5 of 5

Connect user value to business value

Strong healthtech products create a useful experience and a credible financial reason to adopt it.

Patient valueBetter access, outcomes, confidence, or affordability
Provider valueLess burden, better decisions, or more capacity
Payer valueLower avoidable cost and improved outcomes
Buyer valueAdoption, retention, productivity, or measurable return

Useful product and business measures

AdoptionWhether intended users begin and continue using it.
UtilizationHow often eligible people use a service.
OutcomeThe measurable health or operational result.
Return on investmentValue created relative to cost.

Build a credible value chain

A value chain explains how a product interaction is expected to produce an outcome worth paying for. For example, clearer scheduling guidance may increase completed appointments, which may improve timely treatment, which may reduce avoidable high-cost care. Each link is an assumption that should be measured.

Avoid treating engagement as the final outcome. Opening an app, completing an assessment, or sending a message matters only when it contributes to a meaningful patient, clinical, operational, or financial result.

Balance the measurement portfolio

ExperienceCan people understand and complete the intended task?
BehaviorDoes the product help people take the desired action?
OutcomeDoes that action improve health or operations?
EconomicsDoes the value justify the cost of delivering it?
Real-world example

A care-navigation product may measure successful appointments for users, reduced avoidable costs for payers, and benefit engagement for employers.

Design principle

Define experience metrics and business metrics together. A successful flow should improve the user journey and the outcome someone pays for.

Designer takeawayPair every experience metric with the business or health outcome it is expected to influence.

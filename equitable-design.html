Lesson 1 of 5

Exclusion creates health and safety risk

A product can work as designed and still fail people whose bodies, language, resources, technology, or life conditions differ from the assumptions built into it.

The core idea

Safety, accessibility, and equity are connected. When people cannot perceive, understand, operate, afford, or trust an experience, they may lose access to care or make decisions without essential information.

AssumptionWho is expected?
→
BarrierWho is blocked?

ConsequenceWhat harm follows?

Common sources of exclusion

InteractionPeople cannot see, hear, navigate, enter, or confirm information.
UnderstandingLanguage, terminology, or complexity obscures meaning.
AccessCost, connectivity, transportation, time, or eligibility blocks use.
TrustPast harm, unclear practices, or poor representation discourages participation.
Illustrative example

A video-only explanation without captions, transcript, or written next steps can exclude Deaf users, people in noisy environments, and anyone with limited bandwidth.

Designer takeawayTreat exclusion as a failure mode with measurable consequences.
Lesson 2 of 5

Accessibility must be built into the system

Accessible products support people with disabilities through content, interaction, technology compatibility, and reliable alternatives.

PerceivableInformation can be sensed

Alternatives support different ways of perceiving content

OperableInteraction can be completed

People can navigate and act with different inputs

UnderstandableContent and behavior make sense

Language and interactions are clear and predictable

RobustTechnology remains compatible

Assistive technologies can interpret the experience

Conformance is a baseline, not the entire practice

WCAG provides testable criteria for digital accessibility, but checking criteria alone does not reveal every barrier in a complex healthcare journey. Teams should combine standards reviews, automated checks, manual testing, and research with disabled people.

Accessibility also depends on surrounding artifacts and services: PDFs, messages, call centers, identity verification, devices, kiosks, and third-party tools.

Build reusable accessible patterns

StructureUse meaningful headings, labels, relationships, and reading order.
InteractionSupport keyboard use, focus visibility, error recovery, and sufficient time.
PresentationPreserve contrast, resizing, alternatives, and non-color cues.
TestingVerify with assistive technology and people using it.
Designer takeawayMake accessible behavior part of components, content standards, and release criteria.
Lesson 3 of 5

Make health information understandable and usable

Health literacy depends on both individual skills and how well organizations make information and services understandable, navigable, and actionable.

Plain languageUse familiar words and explain necessary technical terms
ActionMake the next step specific and visible
LanguageProvide qualified language support and meaningful translation
ConfirmationCheck understanding rather than assuming it

Clarity is not oversimplification

People need enough information to make an informed decision without unnecessary complexity. Good content prioritizes what matters now, explains risks and uncertainty, and makes deeper detail available.

Translation is not a final text-replacement step. Language access affects layouts, terminology, workflows, support, consent, and whether qualified interpreters or translated materials are available at the right moment.

Illustrative example

“Your result is abnormal” creates anxiety without direction. A clearer experience explains what the result may mean, how urgent it is, who will follow up, and what the person should do next.

Designer takeawayDesign information around understanding, decisions, and next actions.
Lesson 4 of 5

Design for unequal real-world conditions

Equity requires examining whether a product’s benefits, burdens, access, and outcomes differ across populations.

ResourcesCost, time, devices, data plans, and transportation
EnvironmentPrivacy, housing, work, caregiving, and safety
SystemsEligibility, coverage, documentation, and institutional barriers
ExperienceDiscrimination, trust, representation, and prior harm

Average performance can hide unequal outcomes

A product may improve overall completion while making access worse for people with limited English proficiency, disabilities, low-bandwidth connections, or less schedule flexibility. Segment measures and investigate who drops out, waits longer, receives less benefit, or carries more work.

Do not treat people experiencing barriers as edge cases. Involve them in framing the problem, setting priorities, evaluating tradeoffs, and interpreting results.

Designer prompt

Who benefits first, who must work hardest, who is missing from the data, and who may be harmed by the default?

Designer takeawayEvaluate distribution of access, burden, and outcomes, not only averages.
Lesson 5 of 5

Test safety and inclusion continuously

Inclusive intentions must become research practices, product requirements, operational controls, and ongoing measures.

Test the whole journey

RecruitmentInclude people affected by the barriers and decisions being studied.
ScenariosTest interruptions, low bandwidth, assistive technology, and high-stress moments.
OperationsVerify support channels, alternatives, escalation, and recovery.
OutcomesMonitor safety, access, burden, and benefit across groups.

Prepare alternatives and recovery

No single channel works for everyone. Products need practical alternatives when automated identity checks fail, a device is unavailable, a translation is unclear, or a digital flow cannot be completed. Recovery paths should preserve dignity and avoid unnecessary delay.

Design principle

Do not ask whether the product works for most people. Ask who it fails, what happens next, and whether the team can detect and reduce that harm.

Designer takeawayMake inclusion and safety observable, testable, and owned.

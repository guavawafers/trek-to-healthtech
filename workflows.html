Clinical Workflows & Care Delivery

Learn how care teams work and how to design products that fit safely into complex, real-world clinical environments.

Lesson 1 of 5

Care is a team sport

A patient’s care is often delivered by many people across different roles, locations, systems, and moments in time.

The core idea

A clinical product rarely supports one isolated person. Every action can create downstream work, information, or risk for another member of the care team.

PatientShares needs
→
NurseAssesses & coordinates
→
ClinicianDiagnoses & treats
→
Care teamSupports follow-up

Common care-team roles

ClinicianDiagnoses, treats, and makes clinical decisions.
NurseAssesses, educates, monitors, and coordinates.
Care coordinatorConnects services and supports follow-through.
Operations staffManage scheduling, access, and administrative work.
Real-world example

A medication refill request may involve a patient, support staff, a nurse, a prescribing clinician, a pharmacy, and an insurer before it is complete.

Why this matters for product designers

Map who creates information, who receives it, who acts on it, and who is accountable if something is missed.

Designer takeawayDesign for the entire chain of responsibility, not only the most visible user.
Lesson 2 of 5

Map the real clinical workflow

A workflow is the sequence of people, actions, decisions, tools, and information required to complete care.

Before careScheduling, intake, eligibility, preparation
During careAssessment, decisions, treatment, documentation
After careOrders, follow-up, results, coordination
Between visitsMonitoring, messaging, self-management

What to capture in a workflow map

TriggerThe event that begins the workflow.
DecisionA choice that changes the next action.
HandoffResponsibility moving between people or teams.
ArtifactInformation or documentation created along the way.
Real-world example

A lab result workflow begins when a result arrives, branches based on severity, creates notifications, and ends only when the right person reviews and acts on it.

Designer prompt

Do not only map the happy path. Observe workarounds, duplicate entry, waiting, uncertainty, and the moments where people leave one system for another.

Designer takeawayMap the current workflow before proposing the future one.
Lesson 3 of 5

Handoffs and interruptions create risk

Clinical work is rarely linear. People switch tasks, respond to urgent needs, and transfer responsibility throughout the day.

HandoffResponsibility or information moves to another person
InterruptionAttention shifts before a task is completed
EscalationA situation requires more urgent or specialized attention
Follow-upAn action must happen later, often by someone else
Real-world example

A nurse is interrupted while reviewing an abnormal result. If the product does not preserve state and ownership, the result may remain unaddressed.

Design principle

Make state, ownership, urgency, and next actions visible. A product should help people safely resume work after an interruption.

Designer takeawayDesign explicit states for assigned, viewed, acted on, and resolved work.
Lesson 4 of 5

Safety changes the design standard

In healthcare, confusing information or a missed action can affect someone’s health, not just their satisfaction.

ClarityReduce ambiguity

Make critical information easy to interpret

VerificationPrevent mistakes

Confirm high-risk actions and identity

TraceabilityShow what happened

Support accountability and auditing

RecoveryHandle failure safely

Help people identify and correct errors

Safety-oriented design patterns

ConstraintPrevents an unsafe action from occurring.
ConfirmationChecks intent before a high-risk action.
AlertDraws attention to clinically meaningful risk.
Audit trailRecords actions for accountability and review.
Real-world example

A medication-ordering tool may require identity verification, show allergy alerts, and preserve an audit trail because errors can directly harm a patient.

The design implication

Use friction intentionally. Removing every click is not always the right goal when an action is irreversible, high-risk, or clinically significant.

Designer takeawayTreat safety as a core experience requirement, not a final compliance review.
Lesson 5 of 5

Design for adoption, not just usability

A usable product can still fail if it adds work, conflicts with established routines, or does not integrate with existing tools.

Workflow fitSupport how care is actually delivered
Time to valueCreate useful outcomes without excessive setup
IntegrationConnect with existing systems and information
TrustMake recommendations and limitations understandable
Real-world example

A clinically useful tool may still fail if clinicians must leave their primary system, re-enter data, or monitor another inbox to use it.

Design principle

The best clinical tool often feels like less work, not another task. Measure adoption alongside usability and outcomes.

Designer takeawayEvaluate how much new work the product creates before asking teams to adopt it.

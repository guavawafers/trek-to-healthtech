Start with the regulatory context

There is no single rulebook for every healthtech product. Applicable obligations depend on who operates the product, what it does, which data it handles, and where it is used.

The core idea

Do not ask only, “Is this health data?” Ask who collected it, from whom, for what purpose, under which relationship, and where it will go next.

Common parts of the regulatory landscape

HIPAAProtects certain health information handled by covered entities and business associates.
FTC authorityCan apply to consumer health products, privacy promises, and certain health-data breaches.
FDA oversightMay apply when a software function meets the definition of a medical device.
Other rulesState laws, contracts, research rules, and specialized confidentiality requirements may also apply.

The same feature can sit in different contexts

A symptom journal offered directly to consumers may have different obligations than the same feature provided on behalf of a hospital. The interface can look identical while the operator, data relationship, contracts, and applicable rules differ.

Regulatory analysis is a cross-functional responsibility. Product teams should involve privacy, security, legal, compliance, clinical, and operational partners before making decisions that are difficult to reverse.

Real-world example

A wellness app is not automatically covered by HIPAA. But it may still have obligations under the FTC Act, the Health Breach Notification Rule, state law, platform rules, and its own privacy promises.

Designer takeawayMap actors, data flows, purposes, and jurisdictions before selecting a compliance pattern.
Lesson 2 of 5

HIPAA protects information in defined relationships

HIPAA is central to U.S. healthcare privacy, but it does not cover every organization or every piece of health-related information.

Covered entityDirectly regulated

Health plans, clearinghouses, and certain healthcare providers

Business associateSupports a covered entity

Handles PHI while performing certain services or functions

PHIProtected information

Identifiable health information held or transmitted in a covered relationship

BAAContractual safeguard

Defines required protections for business-associate work

HIPAA includes several connected rules

Privacy RuleDefines permitted uses and disclosures of PHI and individual rights.
Security RuleRequires safeguards for electronic PHI.
Breach Notification RuleDefines notification duties following certain breaches of unsecured PHI.
Enforcement RuleProvides processes and penalties for noncompliance.

Permitted does not mean unlimited

HIPAA permits many uses and disclosures without an authorization, including treatment, payment, and healthcare operations. Each category has boundaries, and uses outside permitted purposes may require a valid authorization or another legal basis.

The minimum necessary standard generally requires reasonable efforts to limit PHI to what is needed for the purpose. Important exceptions exist, including disclosures for treatment, so teams should confirm the rule that applies to the specific flow.

Designer prompt

For every role and screen, ask which information is needed for the task, which is merely convenient, and which should not be visible.

Designer takeawayDesign permissions around defined roles, purposes, and relationships, not broad access to “all patient data.”
Lesson 3 of 5

Make permissions and rights usable

Privacy is not achieved by placing a long policy in front of people. Products must make choices, permissions, and consequences understandable throughout the experience.

NoticeExplain relevant data practices clearly and at the right moment
ChoiceOffer meaningful controls where choice is required or promised
AccessHelp people obtain information they are entitled to receive
CorrectionProvide a path to question or amend inaccurate information

Consent, authorization, and permission are not interchangeable

These terms can have different legal meanings depending on the rule and context. A product should not assume that a generic checkbox creates a valid authorization for every use. The workflow must capture the required information and preserve evidence of what the person agreed to.

People also need to understand what happens if they decline, how long a permission lasts, whether they can revoke it, and which uses are necessary to provide the service.

Individual rights create product workflows

RequestMake the right easy to discover and initiate.
IdentityVerify the requester without creating unnecessary barriers.
FulfillmentTrack ownership, deadlines, format, and delivery.
RecordPreserve the request, decision, and response.
Real-world example

A patient requesting records should be able to understand what is available, submit the request, verify identity, follow its status, and receive the information securely.

Designer takeawayTurn privacy rights and permissions into clear, accountable end-to-end workflows.
Lesson 4 of 5

Privacy depends on security and response

A privacy promise is not credible unless the product protects information across collection, access, storage, sharing, and deletion.

AdministrativePolicies, risk analysis, training, and response plans
PhysicalFacilities, devices, workstations, and media
TechnicalAuthentication, authorization, logging, and transmission controls
OperationalMonitoring, escalation, recovery, and learning

Design from a risk analysis

Security controls should respond to the actual information, systems, threats, vulnerabilities, and consequences involved. A risk analysis identifies where sensitive information exists, how it moves, who can access it, and what could go wrong.

Security and usability must be designed together. Controls that people cannot understand or use reliably may create workarounds, while weak controls can expose patients and organizations to serious harm.

Build for detection and response

PreventReduce the chance of inappropriate access or disclosure.
DetectMake unusual or unauthorized activity visible.
RespondRoute incidents to people who can investigate and act.
RecoverRestore service, notify as required, and reduce recurrence.
Real-world example

An analytics or advertising SDK can send sensitive events to a third party. Product teams must understand what every embedded service receives, why it receives it, and whether that disclosure is allowed and accurately described.

Designer takeawayDesign for least privilege, traceability, and incident response, not only the happy path.
Lesson 5 of 5

Build compliance into product decisions

Compliance works best as an ongoing product practice, not a final approval step before launch.

Use a lifecycle approach

CollectRequest only data that has a defined and defensible purpose.
UseRestrict access and secondary use to approved purposes.
ShareUnderstand recipients, contracts, permissions, and onward use.
RetainDefine how long data is needed and how it is safely disposed of.

Maintain an evidence trail

Teams should be able to explain why a feature collects data, which rule or agreement permits each use, who approved the decision, how permissions are enforced, and how the system is monitored. Documentation should remain connected to the implemented product as it changes.

Third-party tools deserve the same scrutiny as first-party features. Analytics, support, identity, messaging, AI, and infrastructure vendors can change the data flow and regulatory context.

A practical review before launch

Data mapDo we know what enters, leaves, and persists?
PurposeCan we explain and defend every use?
ControlAre access, choice, and safeguards implemented?
OperationsCan the team fulfill rights and respond to incidents?
Design principle

A trustworthy product does what it says, says what it does, and can demonstrate both.

Designer takeawayMake the compliant path the natural path for users and internal teams.

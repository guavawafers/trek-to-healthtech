Health data has a lifecycle

Health information is created for a purpose, used by different people, and changed as it moves through care. A data point is never just a value on a screen.

The core idea

Useful health data needs context: who created it, when, why, how it was measured, and whether it is still reliable for the decision being made.

StoreRecord is maintained

ExchangeInformation moves

UseSomeone acts

Common sources of health data

ClinicalDiagnoses, notes, medications, orders, results, and care plans.
AdministrativeScheduling, eligibility, claims, billing, and provider information.
Patient-generatedSymptoms, surveys, goals, device readings, and home measurements.
ContextualSocial, environmental, behavioral, and access-related information.

Every data point reflects a workflow

A blood-pressure result may be entered manually by a medical assistant, imported from a connected device, reported by a patient, or copied from another record. Those values can look identical while carrying different levels of precision and trust.

Before using data in a product, understand how it was generated and what incentives shaped its capture. Information recorded to support billing may not contain the detail needed for a clinical decision.

Real-world example

A medication list may include prescriptions from several clinicians, medications the patient stopped taking, and duplicate entries from imported records. Displaying the list is easy; helping someone determine what the patient actually takes is much harder.

Designer takeawayDesign for the provenance and lifecycle of information, not only its final display.
Lesson 2 of 5

Inside the electronic health record

An EHR is a longitudinal digital record and a working environment used to coordinate care, document decisions, place orders, communicate, and support payment.

Clinical recordHistory, diagnoses, notes, medications, and results
Workflow systemTasks, messages, orders, alerts, and approvals
Operational systemScheduling, registration, capacity, and reporting
Financial systemCoding, charges, claims, and reimbursement support

The record is assembled over time

Different roles add information for different reasons. A clinician documents an assessment, a nurse records an observation, a lab system sends a result, and billing staff may add codes. The EHR brings these pieces together, but they do not automatically form one clear narrative.

EHRs also vary by organization. Local templates, policies, integrations, and workflows can make the same underlying platform behave very differently across care settings.

Structured and unstructured information

Structured dataInformation stored in defined fields that systems can sort and process.
Clinical narrativeFree text that preserves nuance, reasoning, and patient context.
OrderA formal request for a medication, test, referral, or service.
ResultThe information returned after an order or observation is completed.
Real-world example

A diagnosis code can support reporting and billing, while the clinician’s note explains uncertainty, severity, and the reasoning behind the care plan. A useful product may need both.

Designer prompt

When asking for a new field, identify who will enter it, when they will know the answer, how often it changes, and who will maintain it.

Designer takeawayTreat every requested data field as a new workflow and maintenance commitment.
Lesson 3 of 5

Interoperability is more than moving data

Interoperability means systems can exchange information and use it meaningfully. Successful exchange requires technical connection, shared meaning, and workflow fit.

TechnicalCan systems connect?

Data can be sent, received, and accessed

StructuralIs it organized consistently?

Fields and formats can be processed

SemanticDoes it mean the same thing?

Codes and concepts are understood consistently

OrganizationalCan people use it?

Policies and workflows support action

Standards create shared expectations

HL7A family of standards used to exchange and represent health information.
FHIRAn HL7 standard that represents exchangeable information as reusable resources.
TerminologyStandard code systems that help concepts retain consistent meaning.
APIA defined way for software systems to request and exchange information.

A standard does not guarantee a complete experience

FHIR defines common resources and exchange patterns, but implementations still make choices about which fields they support, how information is coded, how often it updates, and what local extensions they use. Two systems can support the same standard and still require careful mapping.

Interoperability also depends on identity matching, permission, trust, and workflow. Data arriving successfully does not help if it reaches the wrong queue, lacks context, or creates more information than a team can review.

Real-world example

A referral can be transmitted electronically, but care still breaks down if the receiving clinic cannot tell why it is urgent, the sending clinician cannot see its status, or the patient does not know what to do next.

Designer takeawayDesign the exchange, interpretation, ownership, and action as one connected experience.
Lesson 4 of 5

Data needs meaning and quality

More data does not automatically create better decisions. Information must be accurate enough, complete enough, current enough, and relevant to the task.

AccuracyDoes the information correctly represent what happened?
CompletenessAre the necessary details present?
TimelinessIs the information current enough for this decision?
RelevanceIs it useful in this role and moment?

Common causes of poor data quality

Missing contextA value appears without source, date, units, or reason.
DuplicationThe same concept appears multiple times with slight differences.
Workflow pressurePeople use shortcuts because accurate capture is too burdensome.
Stale informationData remains visible after the real-world situation changes.

Uncertainty should remain visible

Healthcare information often contains uncertainty: a diagnosis may be suspected, a medication list may be unverified, or an external result may be incomplete. Interfaces that present uncertain information as definitive can create false confidence.

Good design helps people distinguish source, status, recency, and confidence. It also creates a practical path for correcting errors without silently overwriting clinically important history.

Real-world example

A “penicillin allergy” entry may represent a severe reaction, a childhood rash, an unknown family report, or an error. Those distinctions affect clinical decisions and should not be flattened into a simple yes-or-no label.

Designer takeawayMake uncertainty, source, status, and recency understandable at the point of decision.
Lesson 5 of 5

Design useful information, not data dumps

A strong health-data experience helps the right person understand what matters, decide what to do, and trust the information enough to act.

Start with the decision

Do not begin by asking which fields should appear on a screen. Begin with the decision or action the user must complete, then identify the minimum information needed, the context required to interpret it, and the path to investigate further.

Different roles need different views of the same information. A patient may need a plain-language explanation and next steps, while a clinician may need trends, units, reference ranges, and related history.

Design responsibilities around data

CaptureMake accurate entry fit naturally into the workflow.
InterpretProvide context without hiding uncertainty or nuance.
ActConnect information to an owner and a clear next step.
CorrectSupport transparent updates while preserving important history.

Watch for information burden

Adding more data can increase cognitive load, documentation work, alerts, and maintenance. Prioritize rather than merely aggregate. Summaries should remain traceable to source information so people can verify details when needed.

Real-world example

A longitudinal lab view can highlight meaningful change over time, but it should preserve units, reference ranges, collection dates, and source details so a clinician can interpret the trend safely.

Design principle

The goal is not to show everything. The goal is to support a responsible decision while making deeper context available.

Designer takeawayOrganize health data around decisions, actions, and accountability.

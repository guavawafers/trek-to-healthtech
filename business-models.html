Lesson 1 of 5

Start with the value exchange

A business model explains who receives value, who pays, what must happen to deliver the service, and why the arrangement can continue.

The core idea

Healthtech products must create value for the person using them and for the organizations whose money, workflow, authority, or risk make adoption possible.

→
ExperienceValue delivered

RevenueValue captured

Map four kinds of value

User valueBetter access, understanding, convenience, care, or outcomes.
Clinical valueSafer decisions, better coordination, or improved care quality.
Operational valueLess work, greater capacity, or more reliable workflows.
Financial valueRevenue, savings, risk reduction, or stronger performance.
Illustrative example

A virtual-care service may give patients faster access, help clinicians manage demand, and help a purchaser reduce avoidable high-cost care. The experience must support all three claims.

Designer takeawayMake each stakeholder’s promised value and required behavior explicit.
Lesson 2 of 5

The user, customer, and payer may differ

Healthtech companies often serve one person, sell to another organization, and depend on a third party for reimbursement or distribution.

B2CSell to individuals

People choose and pay directly

B2BSell to organizations

Employers, providers, or other businesses buy

B2B2CReach people through buyers

An organization purchases access for members

Reimbursed carePayment follows care rules

Coverage and claims affect revenue

Every model changes the product

A direct-to-consumer product must earn individual trust and willingness to pay. An employer-sponsored product must also support eligibility, reporting, procurement, and measurable buyer value. A reimbursed clinical service must fit documentation, coding, coverage, and care-delivery requirements.

These are not only sales decisions. They change onboarding, permissions, workflows, metrics, support, and the promises the product can responsibly make.

Designer prompt

Whose approval gets the product purchased, whose behavior makes it work, and who experiences the consequences when it fails?

Designer takeawayDesign the full customer system, not only the end-user interface.
Lesson 3 of 5

Revenue follows different rules

How a company earns revenue shapes which behaviors, evidence, and operations the product must support.

SubscriptionRecurring access fee from an individual or organization
Per memberPayment based on the eligible or enrolled population
Per servicePayment tied to visits, tests, transactions, or other activity
PerformancePayment linked partly to outcomes, quality, savings, or risk

Revenue incentives can shape experience

Activity-based revenue may reward more transactions, while performance-based arrangements may reward prevention, coordination, and outcomes. Subscription models require sustained perceived value even when people use the product infrequently.

No model automatically produces good care. Teams should identify where financial incentives align with user outcomes and where they could create pressure, waste, exclusion, or unintended behavior.

Illustrative example

A product paid per completed consultation may optimize appointment conversion. A product paid to improve population outcomes may invest more heavily in outreach, follow-up, and sustained engagement.

Designer takeawayUnderstand which product events create revenue and which create genuine value.
Lesson 4 of 5

Adoption is part of the business model

A product creates no value if buyers approve it but people cannot discover, trust, adopt, or integrate it into real work.

AcquireReach the right buyer and users
ActivateHelp people reach first value
IntegrateFit workflows, systems, and responsibilities
RetainContinue delivering credible value

Go-to-market creates product requirements

Enterprise products may need configurable workflows, security reviews, implementation support, integrations, training, and reporting. Consumer products may need understandable pricing, low-friction onboarding, ongoing engagement, and support for cancellations or changing needs.

Measure the entire adoption funnel. A strong signup rate can hide poor clinical follow-through, low benefit utilization, implementation delays, or burdens shifted to frontline teams.

Designer takeawayTreat discovery, implementation, and ongoing operations as parts of the product experience.
Lesson 5 of 5

Sustainable value requires proof and operations

A compelling interface is only one part of a viable healthtech product. The organization must reliably deliver, measure, and improve the promised outcome.

Test the operating model

CapabilityWhat people, licenses, systems, and partnerships are required?
CostWhat work and infrastructure grow with usage?
EvidenceWhat proves value to users, buyers, and payers?
RiskWhat safety, regulatory, financial, or reputational exposure exists?

Watch for value extraction

A business can grow while creating burden elsewhere: unpaid patient work, extra clinician documentation, inaccessible enrollment, or narrow metrics that exclude complex cases. Sustainable models account for who performs the work and who receives the benefit.

Design principle

The strongest healthtech business models align a useful experience, responsible operations, measurable outcomes, and a credible reason to pay.

Designer takeawayDesign experiences that make sustainable value creation possible and visible.

Lesson 1 of 5

Define success as a meaningful change

Product activity is not the same as healthcare success. A useful outcome describes a meaningful change in health, care, experience, access, safety, or cost.

The core idea

Begin with the outcome that matters, then work backward to the behaviors, workflows, and product capabilities that could responsibly influence it.

NeedWhat should improve?
→
BehaviorWhat must change?
→
ProductWhat can help?
→
OutcomeDid it matter?

Different levels of success

ProductPeople can discover, use, and complete the intended experience.
OperationalTeams deliver work reliably, safely, and efficiently.
ClinicalHealth status, symptoms, function, or care quality changes.
PopulationAccess, outcomes, equity, or cost changes across groups.
Illustrative example

Opening a medication reminder is product activity. Taking medication as intended is behavior. Improved disease control may be an outcome. Each requires different evidence.

Designer takeawayDo not substitute engagement metrics for meaningful outcomes.
Lesson 2 of 5

Use a balanced set of measures

No single metric explains whether a healthcare product works well. Measures should cover what was done, what changed, and what unintended effects occurred.

StructureCapacity and conditions

People, systems, tools, and resources available

ProcessWhat care or work occurred

Actions completed as intended

OutcomeWhat changed

Health, function, experience, access, or cost

BalancingWhat worsened elsewhere

Burden, delays, safety, or unequal effects

Define measures precisely

A measure needs a clear population, event or concept, data source, time period, and calculation. Teams should understand missing data, measurement burden, and whether the measure is valid for the decision being made.

Patient-reported outcomes can capture symptoms, function, or quality of life directly from patients. Experience measures describe interactions with care. Neither should be confused with satisfaction alone.

Designer prompt

What behavior could improve the target metric without improving the underlying outcome?

Designer takeawayPair target measures with balancing measures and data-quality checks.
Lesson 3 of 5

Connect the product to the outcome

Products rarely cause healthcare outcomes alone. A credible measurement plan explains the chain of contribution and the assumptions around it.

Build a theory of change

InputsPeople, technology, data, and resources required.
ActivitiesWhat the product and care team do.
OutputsImmediate evidence that activities occurred.
OutcomesShort- and long-term meaningful changes.

Make assumptions testable

A scheduling tool may improve access only if appointments exist, people can complete booking, transportation or connectivity is available, and care is delivered. The product should measure critical steps and external conditions rather than claiming the final outcome from a single click.

Identify the product’s sphere of control, sphere of influence, and outcomes that depend heavily on other actors or conditions.

Illustrative example

A referral product can measure whether a referral was received, scheduled, completed, and followed up. Improved health may depend on the care delivered afterward and should be evaluated accordingly.

Designer takeawayMake the pathway from feature to outcome explicit and measurable.
Lesson 4 of 5

Interpret evidence carefully

A change in a metric does not automatically prove that the product caused it or that every group benefited.

BaselineWhat was happening before the change?
ComparisonWhat would likely have happened without it?
VariationHow do results differ across people and settings?
DurationDoes the effect persist, fade, or create later consequences?

Look for alternative explanations

Selection effects, seasonality, policy changes, staffing, incentives, documentation changes, and missing data can all change measured results. Strong evaluations match the method to the decision and clearly communicate uncertainty and limitations.

Segment results to identify unequal access, burden, performance, and outcomes. An overall improvement can coexist with harm or worsening outcomes for a smaller population.

Design principle

Measure enough to learn responsibly, but do not turn uncertain associations into confident product claims.

Designer takeawayCommunicate what the evidence supports, what it does not, and for whom.
Lesson 5 of 5

Build a responsible learning loop

Measurement creates value when teams use it to make decisions, improve the product, and monitor unintended consequences.

ObserveCollect useful quantitative and qualitative signals
InterpretReview context, variation, and uncertainty
ActMake an owned product or operational decision
ReassessVerify whether the change helped

Design measurement into operations

Define who reviews each measure, how often, which thresholds trigger action, and who investigates safety or equity concerns. Pair dashboards with research, complaints, support signals, incident reviews, and frontline observations.

Measurement also creates burden and privacy risk. Collect information because it supports a defined decision, and retire measures that no longer justify their cost.

A practical measurement portfolio

ReachWho can access and begins the experience?
QualityIs the intended service delivered reliably and safely?
OutcomeDoes a meaningful result improve?
EquityHow are access, burden, and outcomes distributed?
Designer takeawayConnect every important measure to an owner and a decision.

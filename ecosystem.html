Lesson 1 of 5

How the U.S. healthcare system works

Healthcare is not one system. It is a network of organizations exchanging care, money, information, and rules.

The core idea

A healthtech product rarely serves just one user. Its experience and success are shaped by everyone who delivers, pays for, regulates, or receives care.

PatientReceives care
→
ProviderDelivers care
→
PayerPays claims
→
RegulatorSets rules

Four forces moving through the system

CareServices delivered to prevent, diagnose, or treat health needs.
MoneyPremiums, patient payments, claims, and reimbursement.
InformationRecords, results, orders, and decisions shared across organizations.
RulesCoverage policies, privacy requirements, and clinical standards.
Real-world example

A patient books a virtual visit. A clinician delivers care, documents it in an EHR, and submits information used by an insurer to determine payment. The experience crosses several systems even though it feels like one appointment to the patient.

Why this matters for product designers

Before designing, identify who uses the product, who chooses it, who pays for it, and whose outcomes determine success. These may all be different people.

Designer takeawayMap care, money, information, and rules before mapping screens.
Lesson 2 of 5

Meet the key stakeholders

Select each stakeholder to understand their goals, pressures, and influence on the product experience.

Patients

Need understandable, affordable, and accessible care

Patients navigate symptoms, appointments, costs, treatment, and follow-up while often feeling vulnerable or uncertain.

Design focus: clarity, trust, access, and agency

Stakeholders influence different parts of the product

PatientExperiences care and its outcomes.
ProviderDelivers care and creates clinical information.
PayerDefines coverage and pays for eligible services.
PurchaserChooses or funds benefits, often an employer or government.
Real-world example

A therapy platform may be used by patients, purchased by employers, clinically supported by therapists, and evaluated by health plans. Each audience has a different definition of success.

Designer takeawayResearch the people who influence adoption, not only the person touching the interface.
Lesson 3 of 5

Care settings shape the experience

The same person may move through multiple settings, each with different workflows, urgency, and information needs.

HomeSelf-management, monitoring, virtual care
Primary carePrevention, diagnosis, coordination
Specialty careComplex conditions and treatment
HospitalAcute, high-risk, team-based care

Context changes design needs

UrgencyHow quickly someone needs to understand and act.
ContinuityHow information follows a person across care settings.
CapacityThe time, attention, and resources available to deliver care.
AcuityThe severity and complexity of a patient’s condition.
Real-world example

A symptom questionnaire completed calmly at home may become a concise triage summary for a busy nurse and an urgent alert for an emergency care team. One set of information serves different contexts.

Designer prompt

What happens before and after someone touches your product? The surrounding journey often determines whether the experience works.

Designer takeawayDesign for the transitions between settings, not only isolated moments.
Lesson 4 of 5

The user is not always the buyer

Healthcare products often succeed by meeting the needs of several audiences at once.

UserUses the product

A clinician documenting a visit

BuyerApproves the purchase

A health system executive

BeneficiaryReceives the outcome

A patient receiving care

Decision-makerInfluences adoption

An IT or compliance leader

Why products must create layered value

A product may need to save a clinician time, improve a patient outcome, meet an IT security requirement, and demonstrate financial value to an executive. If one critical audience is ignored, adoption can stall.

Real-world example

An AI documentation tool is used by clinicians, purchased by a health system, reviewed by security and compliance teams, and ultimately intended to improve the patient-clinician interaction.

Designer takeawayCreate a value proposition and success metric for every critical audience.
Lesson 5 of 5

Incentives create design constraints

Every stakeholder defines value differently, creating tensions the product must navigate.

PatientWants affordable, convenient, high-quality care
ProviderWants better outcomes with less administrative burden
PayerWants necessary care delivered at a sustainable cost
RegulatorWants safety, privacy, access, and accountability

Common ecosystem tensions

Access vs. capacityMore demand can overwhelm limited care teams.
Convenience vs. safetyFaster flows may require thoughtful safeguards.
Personalization vs. privacyUseful experiences often require sensitive data.
Quality vs. costBetter outcomes must be financially sustainable.
Real-world example

Removing appointment friction may increase access, but it can also create no-shows or route people to the wrong level of care. Good design considers both the patient experience and system capacity.

Design principle

A strong healthtech experience does not eliminate every tension. It makes tradeoffs understandable and aligns incentives where possible.

Designer takeawayName the tradeoff explicitly and decide whose needs take priority in each moment.
